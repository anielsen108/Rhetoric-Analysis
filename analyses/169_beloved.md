## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Personification** (per-sah-nuh-fuh-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Human qualities to abstractions.
**Evidence:** "was spiteful"
**Effect:** Makes address into malevolent entity.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.

### **Oxymoron** (ahk-see-MOR-ahn)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Contradictory terms.
**Evidence:** "baby's venom"
**Effect:** Combines innocence with poison.
**Near-miss:** Paradox is a contradictory *statement* that unfolds; oxymoron is the two-word spark.

### **Metonymy** (meh-TAH-nuh-mee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Associated term for thing.
**Evidence:** "124"
**Effect:** Reduces haunted house to street number.
**Near-miss:** Metaphor maps by resemblance; metonymy by association. Synecdoche is the special case where the substitute is a *part* of the whole.

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Parataxis** (pair-uh-TAK-sis / ˌpærəˈtæksɪs)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Independent units placed beside one another with minimal explicit hierarchy.
**Evidence:** "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom."
**Effect:** The adjacent statements make the reader infer their relation, giving the transition pressure without over-explaining it.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
