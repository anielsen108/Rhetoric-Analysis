# Passage 094: "We tell ourselves stories" (*The White Album*)

**Joan Didion** · *The White Album* · 1979 | Opening sentences

**Occasion/Pressure:** Frame essay with thesis about narrative as survival mechanism while hinting at its breakdown.
**Persona/Stance:** First-person plural narrator, cool and analytic.
**Thesis of Effect:** Declarative aphorism followed by paratactic story fragments demonstrate both the need for narratives and their failure to save us.

---

## PASSAGE

[L1] We tell ourselves stories in order to live.
[L2] The princess is caged in the consulate.
[L3] The man with the candy will lead the children into the sea.

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Aphorism**
**Evidence:** L1 presents general truth.
**Effect:** Establishes manifesto tone.

### **Allusion**
**Evidence:** Fairy-tale prince/princess, Pied Piper echoes.
**Effect:** Taps archetypal narratives we rely on.

### **Irony**
**Evidence:** Stories meant to sustain involve captivity and doom.
**Effect:** Suggests narratives can betray us.

### **Parataxis**
**Evidence:** Juxtaposed sentences without explanation.
**Effect:** Fragmentation mirrors fractured reality.

---

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Infinitive of purpose**
**Evidence:** "in order to live."
**Effect:** Storytelling framed as survival strategy.

### **Passive voice**
**Evidence:** "princess is caged."
**Effect:** Emphasizes helplessness within story.

### **Future tense**
**Evidence:** "will lead."
**Effect:** Threat projected forward—inevitable outcome.

### **Article repetition**
**Evidence:** "The princess… The man…"
**Effect:** Anaphoric rhythm, like list of case studies.

---

## C) MODERN SYNTACTICAL APPROACHES USED

### **First-person plural reflexive**
**Signals:** "We…ourselves."
**Effect:** Collective responsibility for self-generated myths.

### **Sentence fragments as parables**
**Signals:** Independent clauses functioning as story summaries.
**Effect:** Each line evokes larger narrative unseen.

### **Minimal connective tissue**
**Signals:** Lack of conjunctions.
**Effect:** Highlights disjointed worldview of late-60s Los Angeles.

---

## 1) EAR & PROSODY

**Mouthfeel:** Short declaratives; hard consonants (caged, candy) puncture smoothness.

**Cadence:** First sentence steady; second abrupt; third ominously elongated.

**Music:** Rhythm of fairy tale incantation turning sinister.

---

## 2) SYNTAX AS STYLE (Tufte-grade)

**Sentence shape:** Simple declaratives; first includes purpose phrase, others bare statements.

**Modification:** Sparse; nouns carry weight.

**Information flow:** Thesis → example of containment → example of seduction to destruction.

**Micro-rewrites:**
- **Compressed:** Already minimal.
- **Dilated:** *"We tell ourselves stories so we can go on. In one the princess languishes in a consulate cage. In another a man with candy lures children to the sea."* — Clarifies but softens chill.

---

## 3) DEIXIS, ASPECT, MODALITY

**Deixis:** "We" includes writer and reader; "the" introduces archetypal characters.

**Aspect:** Present habitual in L1 and L2; simple future in L3 implies certainty.

**Modality:** None; statements absolute.

**Temporal logic:** Stories span present need and future consequences.

---

**Metaphor families:**
1. **Captivity** — princess caged.

**Lexical fields:** Storytelling, innocence, danger.

**Image logic:** Narratives we rely on may contain betrayal and violence.

## 9) REVISION STUDIO

**Subtraction test:** Remove middle sentence—lose stepwise intensification.

**Amplification test:** Add connectors—would weaken stark impact.

**Register shift:**
- **Formal:** *"Human beings narrate to themselves in order to endure."*
- **Colloquial:** *"We make up stories so we can get by."*

**Punctuation swap:** Replace periods with commas; would blur discrete fragments.

---

## 10) IMITATIO / COUNTER-IMITATIO

**Imitatio:** *We tell ourselves stories to sleep at night. The hero is trapped in the cul-de-sac. The woman with the key will lock the doors behind us.*

**Counter-Imitatio:** *We tell stories. They are dangerous.* — Lacks resonance.

**Compression (≤25 words):** Already minimal.

---

## 11) STEAL THIS (Takeaways)

1. Open essays with aphorism to set argument.
2. Use parataxis to juxtapose conflicting narratives.
3. Invoke archetypes to tap collective unconscious.
4. Contrast life-preserving thesis with sinister examples.
5. Employ first-person plural for shared complicity.
6. End fragment on future tense to leave lingering dread.
7. Keep sentences short for knife-like precision.

---

## 12) MINI-GLOSSARY (Pronunciation Recap)

**Aphorism** — Concise statement of principle.
**Parataxis** — Placement of clauses without conjunctions.
**Allusion** — Indirect reference to another text or myth.
**Infinitive of purpose** — "to" + verb indicating intent.
**Passive voice** — Subject acted upon ("princess is caged").
