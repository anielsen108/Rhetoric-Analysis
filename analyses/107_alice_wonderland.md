# Passage 107: Down the Rabbit Hole

**Lewis Carroll** · *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* · 1865 | Selected passage

**Occasion/Pressure:** This narrative passage must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.
**Persona/Stance:** A third-person or impersonal voice controls what the reader can know and how quickly the scene or proposition comes into focus.
**Thesis of Effect:** Through inverted syntax mimicking fall, emphatic negative participle, and embedded indirect question, Carroll creates grammar of impulsive adventure—syntax that performs Alice's headlong plunge into illogic.

## PASSAGE

[L1] In another moment down went Alice after it, never once considering how in the world she was to get out again.

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Litotes** (LY-tuh-teez)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Understatement through negation.
**Evidence:** "never once considering"
**Effect:** Emphasizes Alice's impulsiveness through negative construction.
**Near-miss:** Meiosis is plain understatement without the negation mechanism; litotes specifically runs on negatives.

### **Hyperbaton** (hy-PER-buh-tahn)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Inverted word order.
**Evidence:** "down went Alice"
**Effect:** Mimics the inversion of falling; creates fairy-tale syntax.
**Near-miss:** Anastrophe is the mild, single-pair inversion; hyperbaton covers more violent or extended derangements.

### **Tmesis** (t-MEE-sis)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Phrase split by intervening word.
**Evidence:** "how in the world"
**Effect:** Creates colloquial speech pattern; emphasizes bewilderment.
**Near-miss:** Infixation is the linguist's term for the same surgery; parenthesis interrupts a sentence, tmesis interrupts a single word.
