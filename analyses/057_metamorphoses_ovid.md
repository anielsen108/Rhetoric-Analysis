# Passage 057: Ovid's Metamorphoses Opening

## Author and Source
**Ovid**, *Metamorphoses* (translated by A.D. Melville, 8 CE), Book I

## Passage
> Now I am ready to tell how bodies are changed
> Into different bodies.
> I summon the supernatural beings
> who first contrived
> The transformations in the stuff of life.

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Invocation** (in-voh-KAY-shun)
*Definition:* Calling upon higher powers for inspiration.
*Example:* "I summon the supernatural beings"
*Effect:* Claims divine authority for the narrative; follows epic convention.

### **Metalepsis** (met-uh-LEP-sis)
*Definition:* Reference to creative process itself.
*Example:* "Now I am ready to tell"—narrator discusses act of narration.
*Effect:* Makes storytelling itself part of the story; self-reflexive.

### **Synecdoche** (sin-EK-duh-kee)
*Definition:* Part for whole or whole for part.
*Example:* "bodies" representing entire beings; "stuff of life" for matter/existence.
*Effect:* Makes transformations about physical essence, not just appearance.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Temporal Opening
"Now I am ready to tell"—the **temporal adverb "Now"** creates immediacy. After all preparation (reading, invocati etc.), NOW the telling begins.

The **present tense "am ready"** + **infinitive "to tell"** expresses preparedness to act. The syntax makes this a threshold moment.

### First Person
"I am...I summon"—the **first person singular** makes this personal testimony. Unlike Homer's "Sing, goddess" (imperative to muse), Ovid uses "I," claiming agency while still invoking powers.

### Infinitive Complement
"to tell how bodies are changed / Into different bodies"—the **infinitive phrase** explains what he's ready to do. The **noun clause** beginning with "how" specifies content: not what changed, but HOW.

### Passive Construction
"bodies are changed"—the **passive voice** emphasizes the transformation itself, not the agent. The syntax focuses on the event: change happens to bodies.

### Prepositional Phrase
"Into different bodies"—the **preposition "Into"** indicates result/destination. The **modifier "different"** is key—not better or worse, just different. The syntax through modifier choice suggests infinite possibility.

### Line Break
"changed / Into different bodies"—the **enjambment** creates pause on "changed" before we learn the destination. The syntax through line break enacts suspense: changed into what?

### Active Voice Shift
"I summon"—shifts from **passive** (bodies are changed) to **active** (I summon). The syntax makes the narrator active participant, not passive observer.

### Object of Summoning
"the supernatural beings"—the **definite article "the"** + **adjective "supernatural"** + **plural noun**. Not "gods" or "muses" but "beings"—the syntax is vaguer, more inclusive than traditional epic invocation.

### Relative Clause
"who first contrived / The transformations"—the **restrictive relative clause** identifies which beings: those who FIRST did this. The **adverb "first"** emphasizes primacy, origin.

The **verb "contrived"** suggests artifice, craft, intention. These aren't natural transformations but designed ones.

### Object of Contriving
"The transformations in the stuff of life"—the **definite article "The"** makes these specific transformations, not just any. The **prepositional phrase "in the stuff of life"** is vague, almost scientific ("stuff") yet philosophical ("life"). The syntax combines colloquial and elevated registers.

### Syntactic Modernity
This translation uses **modern, conversational syntax**: "Now I am ready," "I summon." Classical invocations are usually more formal. The syntax makes ancient text accessible while maintaining ritualistic function.

### Stylistic Effect
Through first-person declaration, passive transformations becoming active invocation, and mixture of colloquial and elevated diction, this translation creates syntax that bridges ancient and modern. The form performs the content: just as bodies transform into different bodies, classical epic transforms into contemporary narrative. The syntax itself metamorphoses—from passive description to active summoning, from past to present, from distant observation to immediate participation. This is grammar as transformation, syntax that enacts the changes it announces.
