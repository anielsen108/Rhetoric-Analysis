## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Apostrophe** (uh-PAH-struh-fee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Direct address to absent or non-human entity.
**Evidence:** "winds, and crack your cheeks! Rage! Blow! You cataracts"
**Effect:** Makes Lear seem to command nature itself; reveals his madness.
**Near-miss:** Direct address speaks to the present audience ('Reader, I married him' sits between the two); invocation is apostrophe petitioning for aid.

### **Imperative** (im-PAIR-uh-tiv)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Command form.
**Evidence:** "Blow,"
**Effect:** Creates urgency and authority; Lear commands even as he loses power.
**Near-miss:** The label depends on the relation between “Blow,” and the surrounding sentence, not on the phrase in isolation.

### **Personification** (per-sah-nif-ih-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Attribution of human qualities to non-human things.
**Evidence:** "cheeks"
**Effect:** Makes natural forces seem conscious, responsive to human will.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.

### **Hyperbole** (hy-PER-buh-lee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Deliberate exaggeration.
**Evidence:** "Blow"
**Effect:** Externalizes Lear's internal chaos; makes rage cosmic.
**Near-miss:** Its opposite pole is understatement/litotes. When exaggeration collapses into the trivial for comic effect, that's bathos.

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Parataxis** (pair-uh-TAK-sis / ˌpærəˈtæksɪs)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Independent units placed beside one another with minimal explicit hierarchy.
**Evidence:** "Blow, winds, and crack your cheeks! Rage!"
**Effect:** The adjacent statements make the reader infer their relation, giving the transition pressure without over-explaining it.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.

### Stylistic Effect
Through stripped imperatives, apostrophe to natural forces, and commanded apocalypse, Shakespeare creates syntax of madness and rage. The form performs Lear's state: commanding what can't be commanded, addressing what can't hear, demanding destruction of everything. This is syntax as loss of reason—grammar that treats inanimate forces as subjects who must obey, while the actual human king has no subjects left. The imperatives pile up powerlessly, revealing through their very syntax that Lear can only command storms, having lost his human kingdom.
