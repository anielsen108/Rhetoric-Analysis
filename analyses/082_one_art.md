# Passage 082: "One Art"

**Elizabeth Bishop** · *Geography III* · 1976 | Selected passage

**Occasion/Pressure:** This lyric passage must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.
**Persona/Stance:** A third-person or impersonal voice controls what the reader can know and how quickly the scene or proposition comes into focus.
**Thesis of Effect:** Through gerund abstraction, negative construction, and verbs of seeming, Bishop creates syntax of defensive minimization—grammar that tries (and fails) to master grief through understatement.

---

## PASSAGE

[L1] The art of losing isn't hard to master;
[L2] so many things seem filled with the intent
[L3] to be lost that their loss is no disaster.

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Litotes** (LY-tuh-teez)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** Understatement through negation.
**Evidence:** "loss is no disaster"
**Effect:** Minimizes loss while actually revealing its magnitude.
**Near-miss:** Meiosis is plain understatement without the negation mechanism; litotes specifically runs on negatives.

### **Personification** (per-sah-nuh-fuh-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Giving human qualities to things.
**Evidence:** "filled with the intent to be lost"
**Effect:** Makes loss seem purposeful, even conspiratorial.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.

### **Irony** (EYE-ruh-nee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Meaning opposite to literal statement.
**Evidence:** "isn't hard to master"
**Effect:** Creates tension between assertion and actual grief.
**Near-miss:** Sarcasm is verbal irony weaponized at a target. Paradox asserts a real contradiction; irony merely means other than it says.

**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
