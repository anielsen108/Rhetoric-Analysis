# Passage 007: Romeo and Juliet "Wherefore Art Thou"

## Author and Source
**William Shakespeare**, *Romeo and Juliet* (c. 1597), Act 2, Scene 2

## Passage
> O Romeo, Romeo, wherefore art thou Romeo?
> Deny thy father and refuse thy name;
> Or, if thou wilt not, be but sworn my love,
> And I'll no longer be a Capulet.

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Apostrophe** (uh-PAH-struh-fee)
*Definition:* Direct address to an absent person or abstract quality.
*Example:* "O Romeo, Romeo"—speaking to Romeo who cannot hear her.
*Effect:* Creates intimacy and dramatic irony (Romeo actually does hear her); reveals private thoughts.

### **Epizeuxis** (ep-ih-ZOOK-sis)
*Definition:* Immediate repetition of a word for emphasis.
*Example:* "Romeo, Romeo"
*Effect:* Intensifies emotion; suggests obsessive focus on the name (and the problem the name represents).

### **Rhetorical Question** (rih-TOR-ih-kul KWES-chun)
*Definition:* Question asked for effect, not requiring an answer.
*Example:* "wherefore art thou Romeo?"
*Effect:* Expresses existential frustration; the question is philosophical rather than literal.

### **Imperative** (im-PAIR-uh-tiv)
*Definition:* Command or request form.
*Example:* "Deny thy father and refuse thy name"
*Effect:* Shows desperation; she's commanding the impossible, revealing the hopelessness of her situation.

### **Antithesis** (an-TIH-thuh-sis)
*Definition:* Balanced opposition of ideas.
*Example:* "Deny...and refuse" vs. "be...sworn"; keeping name vs. rejecting name.
*Effect:* Structures the passage as a clear either/or choice, emphasizing the tragic necessity of choosing.

### **Parallelism** (par-uh-LEL-iz-um)
*Definition:* Similar grammatical structures in succession.
*Example:* "Deny thy father and refuse thy name"—parallel imperative verbs.
*Effect:* Creates rhythmic insistence; the form suggests logical progression though the content is desperate.

### **Conditional Construction** (kun-DISH-uh-nul)
*Definition:* If-then logical structure.
*Example:* "if thou wilt not, be but sworn my love"
*Effect:* Suggests rationality and choice in a situation that offers neither; ironic use of logical form for emotional plea.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Opening Exclamation
"O Romeo, Romeo"—the **interjection "O"** followed by **direct address** repeated. This is pure vocative case, calling to someone. The syntax is minimalist, stripped to the act of naming. The repetition makes the name itself the subject of contemplation.

### The Misunderstood Question
"wherefore art thou Romeo?"—modern readers misunderstand "wherefore" as "where," but it means "why." The syntax is **interrogative with inverted word order** (art thou rather than thou art), but the question is **rhetorical and philosophical**: Why are you Romeo (a Montague)? Why must you have that name?

The **archaic inverted syntax** ("art thou" rather than "you are") places emphasis on "Romeo"—the name is in the final, stressed position.

### Parallel Imperatives
"Deny thy father and refuse thy name"—two **imperative verbs** connected by "and." The syntax is coordinate, suggesting these are parallel actions, though they're really aspects of the same action. The parallelism through **polysyndeton** creates insistence.

Note the objects of these verbs:
- "thy father" (person/relationship)
- "thy name" (word/identity)

The syntax moves from the personal (father) to the abstract (name), suggesting the name is separable from the person.

### Conditional Alternative
"Or, if thou wilt not"—the **coordinating conjunction "Or"** introduces an alternative, and the **conditional "if"** makes this a subordinate clause. The main clause following is elliptical—"[if thou wilt not] deny thy father and refuse thy name." The syntax requires us to recover the elided content.

"if thou wilt not"—notice the **modal auxiliary "wilt"** (archaic future/volition). The syntax acknowledges Romeo's agency and choice, though Juliet is making demands.

### Imperative as Alternative
"be but sworn my love"—another **imperative**, but gentler. The word "but" (meaning "only" or "just") is crucial—it minimizes what's being asked. The syntax makes it seem simple: "just be sworn my love." The modifier "but" is doing heavy lifting, trying to make a huge commitment seem small.

Note the grammatical structure: be [adjective/state] [object]. "Sworn my love" is a compressed form of "sworn to be my love" or "sworn as my love."

### Conditional Consequence
"And I'll no longer be a Capulet"—this is the **main clause** completing the conditional started earlier. The syntax is:
**If [condition], [imperative], and [I'll + negative + verb]**

The structure is logical: if you won't do X, then do Y, and I'll do Z. This creates the appearance of negotiation, of rational exchange, though we're really witnessing desperation.

### Contraction and Informality
"I'll" is a **contraction**, creating a more intimate, informal register than "I will." The syntax becomes more personal when Juliet speaks of her own actions.

### Negative Construction
"no longer be"—the **negative adverb "no longer"** with copulative verb creates a transformation: from being to not-being (a Capulet). The syntax mirrors Romeo's proposed transformation (from being to not-being a Montague/Romeo).

### Symmetrical Structure
The passage creates **syntactic symmetry**:
- Romeo denies his name → Juliet will deny hers
- The syntax is: [You] [verb] [family identity] → [I] [verb] [family identity]

This parallel structure suggests equality and mutual sacrifice, though Juliet is the only one actually speaking (and Romeo hasn't agreed to anything).

### The Weight on Names
Every clause focuses on **names and naming**:
- "Romeo" (repeated)
- "thy father... thy name"
- "Romeo" (again)
- "Capulet"

The syntax keeps circling back to the problem of nomenclature. Names are both subjects and objects throughout.

### Pronouns: Thou vs. I
The passage alternates between **second person** ("thou," "thy") and **first person** ("I'll"). This creates intimacy—a private language for two people. The archaic pronouns "thou" and "thy" are notably the intimate forms (as opposed to formal "you"), which Shakespeare's audience would have recognized.

### Syntactic Compression
The entire speech is remarkably compressed. Juliet articulates the play's central conflict in four lines through tight, economical syntax. There's no padding, no elaborate description—just the essential problem and proposed solutions.

### Stylistic Effect
Through vocatives, imperatives, conditionals, and parallel structures, Shakespeare creates syntax that **performs** desperate reasoning. Juliet tries to think her way out of tragedy using logical connectives (or, if, and) and balanced structures, but the syntax's very rationality emphasizes the irrationality of the situation. The form is logical; the content is impossible. This gap between syntactic order and semantic chaos is the tragedy itself. The balanced, clear syntax cannot solve what it articulates so perfectly.
