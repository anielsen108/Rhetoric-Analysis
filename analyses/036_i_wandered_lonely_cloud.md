---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Simile** (SIM-ih-lee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Explicit comparison using "like" or "as."
**Evidence:** "lonely as a cloud"
**Effect:** Creates image of isolated wandering; makes speaker's solitude visual.
**Near-miss:** Drop the 'like' and it becomes metaphor. An epic simile extends the comparison over many lines with its own internal scene.

### **Relative Clause** (REL-uh-tiv klawz)
**Lines:** L2
**Definition:** Subordinate clause modifying noun.
**Evidence:** "That floats on high o'er vales and hills"
**Effect:** Extends and elaborates the cloud simile; creates visual scene.
**Near-miss:** The label depends on the relation between “That floats on high o'er vales and hills” and the surrounding sentence, not on the phrase in isolation.

### **Metonymy** (meh-TAH-nuh-mee)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** Substitution of associated term for thing meant.
**Evidence:** "crowd"
**Effect:** Personifies flowers; makes them seem social, animated.
**Near-miss:** Metaphor maps by resemblance; metonymy by association. Synecdoche is the special case where the substitute is a *part* of the whole.

### **Apposition** (ap-uh-ZIH-shun)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** Placement of explanatory element beside noun.
**Evidence:** "A host, of golden daffodils"
**Effect:** Amplifies the sense of multitude through synonymous restatement.
**Near-miss:** Parenthesis interrupts without renaming; apposition always re-identifies the neighboring noun.

### **Hyperbole** (hy-PER-buh-lee)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** Deliberate exaggeration.
**Evidence:** "all at once"
**Effect:** Emphasizes the dramatic nature of the revelation/vision.
**Near-miss:** Its opposite pole is understatement/litotes. When exaggeration collapses into the trivial for comic effect, that's bathos.

"That floats on high o'er vales and hills"—the **relative pronoun "That"** extends the cloud comparison. The syntax elaborates: not just any cloud, but one floating high over valleys and hills. The verb "floats" suggests aimless drifting, continuing the loneliness theme.

The archaic contraction **"o'er"** (over) creates poetic elevation and maintains meter. The syntax through archaic form creates romantic distance.

### Double Apposition
"a crowd, / A host, of golden daffodils"—the syntax uses two nouns in apposition for the flowers:

The comma between them creates pause, giving each term weight. The syntax through restatement emphasizes multitude.

The **modifier "golden"** is the only color word, making it stand out. The syntax foregrounds this one color detail through position and uniqueness.
