## PASSAGE

[L1] To be, or not to be, that is the question:
[L2] Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
[L3] The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
[L4] Or to take arms against a sea of troubles

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "slings and arrows"
**Effect:** Makes suffering into warfare; troubles into ocean.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.

### **Hyperbole** (hy-PER-buh-lee)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** Exaggeration.
**Evidence:** "sea of troubles"
**Effect:** Intensifies overwhelming nature of suffering.
**Near-miss:** Its opposite pole is understatement/litotes. When exaggeration collapses into the trivial for comic effect, that's bathos.

---

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Antithesis** (an-TIH-thuh-sis)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Balanced opposition.
**Evidence:** "To be, or not to be"
**Effect:** Structures entire soliloquy as choice between opposites.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.

### **Zeugma** (ZOOG-muh)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** One verb governing different objects.
**Evidence:** "to take arms against"
**Effect:** Creates grammatical tension between passive suffering and active resistance.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
