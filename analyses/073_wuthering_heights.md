## PASSAGE

[L1] My love for Linton is like the foliage in the woods: time will change it, I'm well aware, as winter changes the trees. My love for Heathcliff resembles the eternal rocks beneath: a source of little visible delight, but necessary.

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Simile** (SIM-ih-lee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Explicit comparison using "like."
**Evidence:** "like the foliage"
**Effect:** Makes abstract emotion concrete through natural imagery.
**Near-miss:** Drop the 'like' and it becomes metaphor. An epic simile extends the comparison over many lines with its own internal scene.

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "My love for Linton is like the foliage in the woods"
**Effect:** Makes passion elemental, fundamental as geology.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.

---

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Antithesis** (an-TIH-thuh-sis)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Balanced opposition.
**Evidence:** "eternal rocks"
**Effect:** Contrasts two kinds of love through parallel syntax.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
