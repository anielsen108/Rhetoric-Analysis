# Passage 050: The Canterbury Tales Prologue

## Author and Source
**Geoffrey Chaucer**, *The Canterbury Tales* (c. 1387), General Prologue

## Passage
> Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote
> The droghte of March hath perced to the roote,
> And bathed every veyne in swich licour
> Of which vertu engendred is the flour

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Personification** (per-sah-nif-ih-KAY-shun)
*Definition:* Attribution of human qualities to non-human things.
*Example:* April with "his" showers; drought that pierces.
*Effect:* Makes natural forces active agents in seasonal drama.

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
*Definition:* Implicit comparison.
*Example:* Plants have "veins"; rain is "liquor" creating "virtue" that "engenders" flowers.
*Effect:* Makes botanical processes seem almost medical/alchemical.

### **Alliteration** (uh-lit-uh-RAY-shun)
*Definition:* Repetition of initial sounds.
*Example:* "shoures soote"; "droghte...March"
*Effect:* Creates musical quality characteristic of Middle English verse.

### **Archaism** (AR-kay-iz-um)
*Definition:* Use of archaic language forms.
*Example:* "Whan that," "soote," "hath perced," "swich," "veyne"
*Effect:* Creates temporal distance; this is language from another age.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Temporal Subordinate Clause
"Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote"—the **temporal conjunction "Whan that"** (When that) introduces subordinate clause. Middle English uses "that" where Modern English would omit it.

The **subject "Aprill"** with **possessive "his"** (April is masculine) and **object "shoures soote"** (sweet showers) creates the agent.

The **adjective following noun** ("shoures soote" = sweet showers) is both Middle English word order and creates emphasis on "soote."

### Perfect Tense
"hath perced"—the **present perfect** "hath perced" (has pierced) uses Middle English auxiliary. The verb "pierced" is violent for what should be gentle spring rain—syntax makes nature aggressive.

### Object of Piercing
"The droghte of March...to the roote"—the **object** is "the drought of March" (accumulated dryness), and the **prepositional phrase "to the roote"** specifies how deep the piercing goes. The syntax creates complete penetration—to the very root.

### Coordinate Addition
"And bathed every veyne"—the **coordinating conjunction** adds second action. The verb shifts from violent (pierced) to gentle (bathed). The syntax through verb choice shows rain both breaks drought and nourishes.

The noun **"veyne"** (vein) makes botanical conduits anatomical—plants have circulatory systems like animals.

### Prepositional Phrase
"in swich licour"—the phrase "in such liquor" specifies what bathes the veins. "Licour" (liquor/liquid) makes rainwater almost medicinal or alchemical.

### Relative Clause
"Of which vertu engendred is the flour"—this **relative clause** explains the liquor's power. The syntax **inverts normal order**: "engendred is" instead of "is engendred." This inversion is both poetic and emphatic.

The abstract noun **"vertu"** (virtue/power) suggests almost magical property. The verb **"engendred"** (engendered/created) makes plant growth seem like procreation.

### Incomplete Sentence
The passage doesn't complete syntactically—it's one long temporal clause ("When April...") without the main clause (which comes later: "then people long to go on pilgrimage"). This **suspended syntax** creates anticipation—we're waiting for what happens WHEN these conditions are met.

### Stylistic Effect
Through personification, archaic syntax, violent-then-gentle verb progression, and suspended sentence structure, Chaucer creates the most famous opening in Middle English literature. The syntax performs spring's arrival: breaking winter's grip (pierced), nourishing growth (bathed), creating new life (engendred). The form IS the season—syntax as natural process, grammar as seasonal cycle beginning again.
