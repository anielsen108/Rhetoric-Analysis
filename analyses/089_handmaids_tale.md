# Passage 089: "The Handmaid's Tale"

**Margaret Atwood** · *The Handmaid's Tale* · 1985 | Selected passage

**Occasion/Pressure:** This narrative passage must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.
**Persona/Stance:** A direct-address voice controls what the reader can know and how quickly the scene or proposition comes into focus.
**Thesis of Effect:** Through tense uncertainty, rhetorical questioning of idiom, and syntactic reversal of rising/falling, Atwood creates grammar of resistance—language that questions even basic metaphors, refusing to accept conventional descriptions of reality.

---

## PASSAGE

[L1] Night falls. Or has fallen. Why is it that night falls, instead of rising, like the dawn? Yet if you look east, at sunset, you can see night rising, not falling; darkness lifting into the sky, up from the horizon.

---

### **Correctio** (kor-REK-tee-oh)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Self-correction.
**Evidence:** "Night falls. Or has fallen."
**Effect:** Creates uncertainty about time and observation.
**Near-miss:** Aposiopesis abandons the sentence; correctio revises it in flight and keeps going.

### **Rhetorical Question** (rih-TOR-ih-kul KWES-chun)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Question not requiring answer.
**Evidence:** "Why is it that night falls"
**Effect:** Challenges linguistic convention; makes reader reconsider assumptions.
**Near-miss:** Hypophora asks and then *answers its own* question; aporia performs genuine (or staged) doubt.

---

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Antithesis** (an-TIH-thuh-sis)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Balanced opposition.
**Evidence:** "darkness lifting"
**Effect:** Inverts expected metaphor; makes darkness ascend.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
