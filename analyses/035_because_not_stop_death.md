[L2] He kindly stopped for me –

[L4] And Immortality.

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Personification** (per-sah-nif-ih-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L2
**Definition:** Attribution of human qualities to non-human things.
**Evidence:** "Death – He kindly stopped for me – The Carriage"
**Effect:** Transforms terror into courtesy; makes death familiar, even social.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.

### **Irony** (EYE-ruh-nee)
**Lines:** L2
**Definition:** Expression opposite to literal meaning.
**Evidence:** "kindly"
**Effect:** Creates unsettling tone; challenges assumptions about death.
**Near-miss:** Sarcasm is verbal irony weaponized at a target. Paradox asserts a real contradiction; irony merely means other than it says.

### **Litotes** (LIE-tuh-teez)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Understatement using negative construction.
**Evidence:** "could not stop"
**Effect:** Creates genteel, understated tone appropriate for Victorian sensibility.
**Near-miss:** Meiosis is plain understatement without the negation mechanism; litotes specifically runs on negatives.

### **Capitalization** (kap-ih-tul-ih-ZAY-shun)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** Use of capital letters for emphasis or personification.
**Evidence:** "he Carriage"
**Effect:** Elevates concepts to allegorical status; creates formality.
**Near-miss:** The label depends on the relation between “he Carriage” and the surrounding sentence, not on the phrase in isolation.

## 2) SYNTAX AS STYLE (Tufte-grade)

**Sentence architecture:** The excerpt uses 4 displayed lines and 1 sentence movement. Its grammar distributes attention before, within, and after the main clauses instead of treating word order as a neutral container.

### Subordinate Opening
"Because I could not stop for Death"—begins with **subordinate conjunction**, creating dependent clause before independent. The syntax inverts normal order (usually main clause first), creating suspense.

The **modal negative** "could not" expresses inability, not unwillingness. The syntax through modal choice indicates death isn't something we choose.

### Dash as Syntax
Dickinson's signature **em-dashes** create pauses and emphases. The dash after "Death" separates clauses while maintaining connection—her dashes function like both periods and commas simultaneously.

### Chiastic Reversal
"I could not stop for Death - / He kindly stopped for me"—the syntax reverses the action:
- I could not stop for Death
- Death stopped for me

This **chiasmus** (A-B to B-A pattern) creates elegant balance and irony—death accommodates us since we cannot accommodate death.

### Exclusive Pronoun
"The Carriage held but just Ourselves"—the syntax uses "but just" (meaning "only") to limit occupants. The **first person plural "Ourselves"** capitalized suggests special intimacy, exclusivity. This is a private journey.

### Final Abstraction
"And Immortality"—this **sentence fragment** (just a noun phrase) ends the stanza. The syntax's compression is striking—Immortality rides along, no verb, no explanation. The form creates mystery through incompletion.

### Stylistic Effect
Through inverted subordination, dashes, capitalization, and fragments, Dickinson creates her signature compressed, enigmatic syntax. The form performs the content: death is both ending (dashes interrupt) and continuation (sentences don't quite end), both intimate (Ourselves) and abstract (Immortality). Syntax as paradox.
