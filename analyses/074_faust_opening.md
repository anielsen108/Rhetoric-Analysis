# Passage 074: Faust Opening

## Author and Source
**Johann Wolfgang von Goethe**, *Faust* (translated by Walter Kaufmann, 1808), Part I

## Passage
> I have, alas, studied philosophy,
> jurisprudence and medicine, too,
> and, worst of all, theology
> from end to end with diligence.

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Catalog** (KAT-uh-log)
*Definition:* List of related items.
*Example:* Philosophy, jurisprudence, medicine, theology
*Effect:* Accumulates learning to emphasize its futility.

### **Climax** (KLY-maks)
*Definition:* Arrangement from least to most intense.
*Example:* Building to "worst of all, theology"
*Effect:* Makes theology the culmination of wasted study.

### **Interjection** (in-ter-JEK-shun)
*Definition:* Exclamatory word inserted.
*Example:* "alas"
*Effect:* Expresses regret; colors entire statement with sorrow.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Perfect Tense Opening
"I have...studied"—**present perfect tense** connects past action (studying) to present state (still affected by it). Syntax makes past study relevant to current despair.

### Interjection
"alas"—**parenthetical interjection** interrupts syntax to express emotion. Placed after auxiliary but before participle, it colors the entire predicate with regret.

### List Structure
The syntax uses **polysyndeton** (multiple "and"s) to connect fields:
- philosophy
- jurisprudence and medicine
- and...theology

The **commas and conjunctions** create accumulation, each field adding to the burden.

### Superlative Phrase
"worst of all"—**superlative** + prepositional phrase emphasizes theology as most problematic. Syntax through intensifier makes this the climax.

### Prepositional Phrase of Manner
"from end to end with diligence"—**two prepositional phrases** specify how study was conducted. "From end to end" emphasizes completeness; "with diligence" emphasizes effort.

The syntax makes clear: this wasn't superficial study. The thoroughness makes the failure more devastating.

### Inversion
In German original and some translations, word order inverts for emphasis. Even in English, the **placement of "alas"** and "worst of all"** create emphatic disruption of normal syntax.

### Stylistic Effect
Through perfect tense connecting past to present despair, accumulating catalog, and climactic "worst of all," Goethe creates syntax of intellectual disillusion. Form IS the failed quest—grammar that lists achievements only to frame them as failures.
