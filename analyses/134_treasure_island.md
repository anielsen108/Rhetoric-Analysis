## PASSAGE

[L1] Fifteen men on the dead man's chest—Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum! Drink and the devil had done for the rest—Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum!

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Refrain** (rih-FRAYN)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Repeated phrase.
**Evidence:** "Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum!"
**Effect:** Creates chanty rhythm; makes song memorable and ominous.
**Near-miss:** The label depends on the relation between “Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum!” and the surrounding sentence, not on the phrase in isolation.

### **Euphemism** (YOO-fuh-miz-um)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Mild expression for harsh reality.
**Evidence:** "done for"
**Effect:** Creates casual, callous tone toward death.
**Near-miss:** Periphrasis is roundabout wording for any reason; euphemism specifically launders the unpleasant.

### **Metonymy** (meh-TAH-nuh-mee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Associated term substituting.
**Evidence:** "Drink and the devil"
**Effect:** Personifies causes of pirate deaths.
**Near-miss:** Metaphor maps by resemblance; metonymy by association. Synecdoche is the special case where the substitute is a *part* of the whole.

---

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Asyndeton** (uh-SIN-duh-tahn)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Omission of conjunctions.
**Evidence:** "Drink and the devil"
**Effect:** Links alcohol and evil as unified force.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
