## PASSAGE

[L1] Imagine a ruin so strange it must never have happened. First, picture the forest. I want you to be its conscience, the eyes in the trees.

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Apostrophe** (uh-PAHS-truh-fee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Direct address to reader.
**Evidence:** "I want you to be"
**Effect:** Creates intimate, commanding relationship with reader.
**Near-miss:** Direct address speaks to the present audience ('Reader, I married him' sits between the two); invocation is apostrophe petitioning for aid.

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "eyes in the trees"
**Effect:** Makes reader into moral witness embedded in landscape.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.

### **Paradox** (PAIR-uh-doks)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Seeming contradiction.
**Evidence:** "so strange it must never have happened"
**Effect:** Suggests trauma too extreme to be credible.
**Near-miss:** Oxymoron is paradox compressed to a two-word collision ('living death'); antithesis balances opposites without asserting both are true.

---

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Parataxis** (pair-uh-TAK-sis / ˌpærəˈtæksɪs)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Independent units placed beside one another with minimal explicit hierarchy.
**Evidence:** "Imagine a ruin so strange it must never have happened. First, picture the forest."
**Effect:** The adjacent statements make the reader infer their relation, giving the transition pressure without over-explaining it.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
