# Passage 084: "The Snow Man"

**Wallace Stevens** · *Harmonium* · 1921 | Selected passage

**Occasion/Pressure:** This lyric passage must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.
**Persona/Stance:** A third-person or impersonal voice controls what the reader can know and how quickly the scene or proposition comes into focus.
**Thesis of Effect:** Through impersonal pronoun, modal necessity, and genitive metaphor, Stevens creates syntax of detached observation—grammar that demands emptying of human warmth to see things as they are.

---

## PASSAGE

[L1] One must have a mind of winter
[L2] To regard the frost and the boughs
[L3] Of the pine-trees crusted with snow

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "mind of winter"
**Effect:** Makes consciousness itself cold, crystalline, detached.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.

### **Synecdoche** (sin-EK-duh-kee)
**Lines:** L2
**Definition:** Part for whole.
**Evidence:** "frost and"
**Effect:** Focuses on specific details to evoke larger scene.
**Near-miss:** A subtype of metonymy: synecdoche requires the part/whole relation specifically, not just any association.

### **Personification** (per-sah-nuh-fuh-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Human qualities to non-human.
**Evidence:** "mind"
**Effect:** Makes season into mode of consciousness.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.
