# Passage 084: "The Snow Man"

## Author and Source
**Wallace Stevens**, *Harmonium* (1921)

## Passage
> One must have a mind of winter
> To regard the frost and the boughs
> Of the pine-trees crusted with snow

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
*Definition:* Implicit comparison.
*Example:* "mind of winter"
*Effect:* Makes consciousness itself cold, crystalline, detached.

### **Synecdoche** (sin-EK-duh-kee)
*Definition:* Part for whole.
*Example:* "frost and boughs" represent entire winter landscape.
*Effect:* Focuses on specific details to evoke larger scene.

### **Personification** (per-sah-nuh-fuh-KAY-shun)
*Definition:* Human qualities to non-human.
*Example:* Winter has a "mind"
*Effect:* Makes season into mode of consciousness.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Impersonal "One"
"One must have"—**impersonal pronoun** with modal auxiliary. Syntax creates philosophical distance, universal statement.

### Modal Necessity
"must have"—**modal verb** expressing requirement. Syntax makes perception conditional on transformation.

### Genitive Metaphor
"mind of winter"—**prepositional phrase of possession** creates unusual genitive. Not a wintry mind, but mind BELONGING TO winter.

### Infinitive Purpose
"To regard"—**infinitive** expresses purpose. Syntax makes the winter-mind necessary FOR proper seeing.

### Participial Modification
"crusted with snow"—**past participle** modifies pine-trees. Syntax makes snow an encrustation, coating perception itself.

### Stylistic Effect
Through impersonal pronoun, modal necessity, and genitive metaphor, Stevens creates syntax of detached observation—grammar that demands emptying of human warmth to see things as they are.
