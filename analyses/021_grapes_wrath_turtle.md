# Passage 021: The Grapes of Wrath - The Turtle

## Author and Source
**John Steinbeck**, *The Grapes of Wrath* (1939), Chapter 3

## Passage
> The sun lay on the grass and warmed it, and in the shade under the grass the insects moved, ants and ant lions to set traps for them, grasshoppers to jump into the air and flick their yellow wings for a second, sow bugs like little armadillos, plodding restlessly on many tender feet.

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Personification** (per-sah-nif-ih-KAY-shun)
*Definition:* Attribution of human qualities to non-human things.
*Example:* The sun "lay" on the grass (as one might lie on a bed).
*Effect:* Creates intimate relationship between natural elements; makes nature active, almost sensual.

### **Parataxis** (pair-uh-TAK-sis)
*Definition:* Coordination without subordination.
*Example:* The accumulation of insect descriptions without hierarchy.
*Effect:* Creates equality among observations; mimics natural proliferation of life.

### **Asyndeton** (uh-SIN-duh-ton)
*Definition:* Omission of conjunctions between elements.
*Example:* "ants and ant lions...grasshoppers...sow bugs"—minimal conjunctions for long list.
*Effect:* Creates accumulative rush; suggests abundance of life.

### **Simile** (SIM-ih-lee)
*Definition:* Explicit comparison using "like" or "as."
*Example:* "sow bugs like little armadillos"
*Effect:* Makes the small seem important through comparison; creates visual precision.

### **Synesthesia** (sin-es-THEE-zhuh)
*Definition:* Mixing of sensory impressions.
*Example:* "warmed" (tactile) combined with visual descriptions of movement and color.
*Effect:* Creates rich sensory experience; engages multiple senses simultaneously.

### **Catalog** (KAT-uh-log)
*Definition:* List or enumeration of related items.
*Example:* The list of insects and their actions.
*Effect:* Creates sense of abundance, diversity, and interconnected life.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Opening Sentence
"The sun lay on the grass and warmed it"—the syntax begins with **subject-verb-prepositional phrase + coordinating conjunction + verb-object** structure. This is Steinbeck's characteristic simple, clear syntax.

The verb **"lay"** is intransitive but used with unusual object (the sun doesn't typically "lie"). The syntax treats the sun as body, the grass as bed. This **unconventional verb choice** within conventional syntax creates fresh perception.

The **coordination "and warmed it"** adds a second action. The syntax connects passive position (lay) with active effect (warmed), suggesting that even passive presence creates change.

The **pronoun "it"** refers to grass—the sun lies ON the grass and warms THE grass. The syntax creates intimacy through this double contact.

### Contrasting Clause
"and in the shade under the grass the insects moved"—this **coordinate independent clause** is linked by "and" to the previous sentence, but introduces contrast: sun above, shade below.

The **prepositional phrases stack**:
- "in the shade" (general location)
- "under the grass" (specific location)

This **syntactic layering** creates precise spatial orientation. We move from above (sun) to below (shade under grass).

The **subject "the insects"** is general, not yet specific. The syntax introduces the category before naming individual members—a strategy that creates anticipation.

The verb **"moved"** is simple, intransitive, suggesting general activity before we get specifics.

### Accumulating Specifics
"ants and ant lions to set traps for them, grasshoppers to jump into the air and flick their yellow wings for a second, sow bugs like little armadillos, plodding restlessly on many tender feet"—this is a **long appositive series** specifying "the insects."

The syntax uses **infinitive phrases** of purpose:
- "to set traps" (purpose of ant lions)
- "to jump" (purpose of grasshoppers)
- "to flick" (purpose continued)

This **purposive syntax** suggests intention, agency, even consciousness in the insects. The grammar treats them as actors with goals.

### First Pair
"ants and ant lions to set traps for them"—the syntax **coordinates** two related insects (ants and their predators, ant lions). The **plural "them"** presumably refers back to ants, though the syntax is slightly ambiguous (does it include ants as prey?).

The phrase **"to set traps"** gives ant lions sophisticated agency—they're not just existing but strategizing. The syntax through verb choice elevates insect behavior.

### Second Group
"grasshoppers to jump into the air and flick their yellow wings for a second"—this section has **more complex syntax**:
- infinitive "to jump"
- prepositional phrase "into the air"
- coordinated infinitive "and flick"
- object "their yellow wings"
- temporal phrase "for a second"

The syntax creates cinematic quality—we see the jump, the flick, the yellow color, the brief duration. The **accumulating prepositional phrases and modifiers** create visual richness.

The verb **"flick"** is precise, suggesting quick, snapping motion. Steinbeck's syntax consistently uses exact verbs rather than general ones.

The modifier **"yellow"** is the only color word, making it stand out. The syntax foregrounds this visual detail.

The phrase **"for a second"** provides temporal precision. The syntax doesn't just describe the action but its duration.

### Third Group
"sow bugs like little armadillos"—this section uses **simile** ("like") to describe. The syntax shifts from purpose infinitives to comparative structure.

The phrase **"little armadillos"** is endearing through the modifier "little." The syntax creates affection through diminutive.

This **comparative syntax** (X like Y) does double work: it clarifies appearance (for those who might not know sow bugs) while also suggesting something charming about both creatures.

### Final Participial Phrase
"plodding restlessly on many tender feet"—this **present participle phrase** modifies "sow bugs" and creates the final image. The syntax uses:
- participle: "plodding" (slow, heavy movement)
- adverb: "restlessly" (contradicting the slow movement—moving slowly but continuously)
- prepositional phrase: "on many tender feet"

The **oxymoronic pairing** "plodding restlessly" is syntactically standard (participle + adverb) but semantically paradoxical. The syntax juxtaposes contradictory qualities, capturing complexity of the movement.

The phrase **"many tender feet"** is remarkable. The adjective "tender" applied to insect feet is unexpected, almost touching. The syntax through word choice creates empathy with these tiny creatures.

### Paratactic Accumulation
The entire sentence after "the insects moved" is essentially **one long appositive** with three main sections separated by commas. The syntax uses minimal coordination:
- Only one "and" joins ants and ant lions
- Commas separate the major groups
- Final participial phrase lacks any connective

This **asyndetic accumulation** creates breathless quality, suggesting the profusion of life. The syntax doesn't impose hierarchy—all these insects are equally present, equally active.

### Purposive vs. Descriptive
Notice the syntax shifts:
- "ants and ant lions" + infinitive of purpose (what they do)
- "grasshoppers" + infinitive of purpose (what they do)
- "sow bugs" + comparison + participle (what they're like, what they're doing)

This **syntactic variation** within parallel structure prevents monotony. Each insect group gets slightly different grammatical treatment.

### Sentence Length
This is a **single long sentence** of 52 words. The syntax sustains itself through coordination and apposition without becoming confused. This **cumulative, additive syntax** is characteristic of Steinbeck—building through addition rather than complex subordination.

### Concrete Diction in Simple Syntax
Every word is **concrete and specific**:
- Not "light" but "sun"
- Not "plants" but "grass"
- Not "bugs" but "ants," "ant lions," "grasshoppers," "sow bugs"
- Not "moved quickly" but "jump," "flick"
- Not "small legs" but "many tender feet"

The syntax uses simple structures filled with precise, concrete nouns and verbs. This is what Tufte would call **syntactic clarity with lexical richness**.

### Spatial Organization
The syntax creates **spatial layers**:
1. Sun (above)
2. Grass (surface)
3. Shade under grass (below surface)
4. Insects in that shade (life in the hidden space)

This **syntactic descent** from sun to shade to insects creates three-dimensional space through grammatical structure.

### Sensory Progression
The syntax moves through senses:
- Tactile: "warmed"
- Visual: "yellow wings," "like little armadillos"
- Kinetic: "moved," "jump," "flick," "plodding"

This **multi-sensory syntax** creates immersive experience. The grammar doesn't just tell but shows and makes us feel.

### Verb Tense
Everything is in **simple past tense**, but it reads as **habitual past** (this is what was always happening, not what happened once). The syntax through consistent past tense creates timeless quality—this is how it was, day after day, in the Oklahoma before the Dust Bowl.

### The General and the Particular
The syntax moves from **general to particular**:
- "the insects" (general category)
- "ants and ant lions...grasshoppers...sow bugs" (specific types)
- Details of what each does (particular actions)

This **syntactic narrowing** is like a camera zooming in, which is cinematic technique embedded in grammar.

### No Human Presence
Notice the syntax contains **no human subjects, no first or second person**. Everything is third-person natural world. This **syntactic exclusion of humans** is strategic—the interchapter describes nature independent of human observation or intervention.

This creates what critics call Steinbeck's "biological perspective"—seeing human events within larger natural context. The syntax enacts this by treating insects with the same grammatical seriousness usually reserved for human actors.

### Stylistic Effect
Through cumulative paratactic syntax, precise concrete diction, and sympathetic attention to small life, Steinbeck creates prose that's both scientifically observant and deeply compassionate. The syntax doesn't subordinate or summarize but accumulates detail after detail, giving each creature its grammatical due.

As Tufte emphasizes, this is **syntax as democratic vision**—the paratactic structure treats ants and grasshoppers and sow bugs as equally worthy of attention, each getting its own clause or phrase. The form embodies Steinbeck's philosophy: all life is interconnected and valuable. The simple syntax filled with precise observation enacts patient, loving attention to the world. This is syntax as ethics, grammar as respect for existence in all its forms. The form IS the content—the non-hierarchical syntax reflects the non-hierarchical value system.
