## PASSAGE

[L1] I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up and live out the true meaning of its creed: "We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men are created equal."

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Allusion** (uh-LOO-zhun)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Reference to another text or historical document.
**Evidence:** "I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up"
**Effect:** Links civil rights movement to founding American principles; claims moral authority.
**Near-miss:** Quotation announces its borrowing; allusion smuggles it. Metalepsis is allusion at a further remove, referencing through a chain.

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "live out"
**Effect:** Makes abstract political entity concrete and active; suggests awakening or resurrection.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.

### **Personification** (per-sah-nif-ih-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Attribution of human qualities to non-human things.
**Evidence:** "will rise up and live out"
**Effect:** Makes national destiny a matter of moral choice and action.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.

**Effect:** Creates rhythmic emphasis; builds momentum; makes the vision personal and prophetic.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.

## 2) SYNTAX AS STYLE (Tufte-grade)

**Sentence architecture:** The excerpt uses 1 displayed line and 1 sentence movement. Its grammar distributes attention before, within, and after the main clauses instead of treating word order as a neutral container.

### First Person Declaration
"I have a dream"—the syntax begins with **first person singular subject + verb + object**. This is remarkably personal for a speech addressing hundreds of thousands. The syntax makes the vision individual before making it collective.

The **present perfect tense** ("have") rather than simple present ("I dream") suggests the dream is ongoing, not momentary—it exists, persists, continues.

### Subordinate Noun Clause
"that one day this nation will rise up"—the **noun clause** introduced by "that" serves as appositive to "dream," specifying its content. The syntax creates a layered structure: I have a dream [which is] that...

The **temporal phrase "one day"** is deliberately vague—not specifying when creates both hope (it will happen) and patience (we don't know when).

### Future Tense
"will rise up and live out"—the **future tense** expresses certainty, not mere hope. The syntax through modal choice asserts the inevitability of justice.

The **two verbs** "rise up" and "live out" create parallel actions:
- "rise up" (awakening, resurrection, elevation)
- "live out" (embodiment, practice, fulfillment)

### Colon and Quotation
The **colon** introduces the embedded quotation from Jefferson. This syntax creates formal citation, lending authority to the vision. King's syntax literally builds on Jefferson's, creating structural unity between 1776 and 1963.

### Stylistic Effect
Through personal declaration, future certainty, and embedded quotation from the Declaration, King creates syntax that is simultaneously intimate (I have a dream) and universal (quoting founding document). The form enacts the content: individual vision grounded in collective American principles. As Tufte would note, the syntax performs the rhetorical work of making revolution sound like patriotism, making radical change sound like fulfillment of original promise.

**Information flow:** The opening establishes “I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up and live out the true meaning of its…”; subsequent material converts that initial footing into the new emphasis carried by “I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up and live out the true meaning of its….”
