# Passage 001: Moby-Dick Opening

## Author and Source
**Herman Melville**, *Moby-Dick; or, The Whale* (1851), Chapter 1

## Passage
> Call me Ishmael. Some years ago—never mind how long precisely—having little or no money in my purse, and nothing particular to interest me on shore, I thought I would sail about a little and see the watery part of the world.

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Anaphora** (uh-NAF-or-uh)
*Definition:* Repetition of words at the beginning of successive clauses.
*Example:* The repeated "and" constructions in the passage.
*Effect:* Creates a sense of accumulation and casual, conversational listing of circumstances.

### **Parallelism** (par-uh-LEL-iz-um)
*Definition:* Similar grammatical structure in related phrases or clauses.
*Example:* "having little or no money" and "nothing particular to interest me"
*Effect:* Establishes rhythm and balance, linking the speaker's poverty with his ennui.

### **Litotes** (LIE-tuh-teez)
*Definition:* Deliberate understatement, often using negative constructions.
*Example:* "little or no money," "nothing particular"
*Effect:* Understates the severity of circumstances, creating a casual, self-deprecating tone.

### **Parenthesis** (puh-REN-thuh-sis)
*Definition:* Insertion of an aside or extra information into a sentence.
*Example:* "never mind how long precisely"
*Effect:* Creates an intimate, conversational tone; establishes the narrator's voice as casual and confiding.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Sentence Pattern
The passage opens with an **imperative fragment** ("Call me Ishmael"), followed by a **loose sentence** that unfolds through accumulation. This is classic Tuftean loose construction—the main clause ("I thought I would sail about a little") comes after an extended series of subordinate elements.

### Syntactic Structure
The syntax moves from **brevity to expansion**: the three-word opening gives way to a leisurely, multi-clause sentence. This mirrors the movement from land to sea, from confinement to freedom. The sentence is built on a **cumulative principle**, adding detail after detail in free modifiers:

- Temporal frame: "Some years ago"
- Temporal vagueness: "never mind how long precisely"
- Economic condition: "having little or no money in my purse"
- Emotional state: "nothing particular to interest me on shore"
- Decision/action: "I thought I would sail about a little and see the watery part of the world"

### Grammatical Choices
The use of **participial phrases** ("having little or no money," "having...nothing particular") creates a sense of circumstances accumulating to necessitate action. The **passive construction implied** in "Call me" (rather than "My name is") immediately establishes the narrator's relationship with the reader as collaborative rather than declarative.

### Rhythm and Pacing
The rhythm shifts dramatically: the opening staccato gives way to a flowing, almost meandering sentence that mimics the motion of water itself. The phrase "sail about a little" with its colloquial understatement contrasts with the more expansive "see the watery part of the world."

### Stylistic Effect
Melville establishes **voice through syntax**: the interruption, the vagueness, the casual phrasing ("sail about a little"), and the understated motivations all characterize Ishmael as an informal, self-aware, slightly ironic narrator. The syntax performs the personality it describes.
