# Passage 085: "Tonight I Can Write"

**Pablo Neruda** · *Twenty Love Poems and a Song of Despair* · 1924 | Selected passage

**Occasion/Pressure:** This lyric passage must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.
**Persona/Stance:** A first-person voice controls what the reader can know and how quickly the scene or proposition comes into focus.
**Thesis of Effect:** Through modal transformation (cannot write → can write), imperative self-address, and nested quotation, Neruda creates syntax of grief becoming poetry—grammar that performs the very composition it describes.

---

## PASSAGE

[L1] Tonight I can write the saddest lines.
[L2] Write, for example, 'The night is starry
[L3] and the stars are blue and shiver in the distance.'

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Apostrophe** (uh-PAH-struh-fee)
**Lines:** L2
**Definition:** Direct address (here to self/reader).
**Evidence:** "Write, for example"
**Effect:** Makes writing process visible; creates intimacy.
**Near-miss:** Direct address speaks to the present audience ('Reader, I married him' sits between the two); invocation is apostrophe petitioning for aid.

### **Personification** (per-sah-nuh-fuh-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** Human qualities to non-human.
**Evidence:** "shiver"
**Effect:** Projects speaker's emotional state onto cosmos.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.

---

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Polyptoton** (pah-LIP-tuh-tahn)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Repetition of word in different forms.
**Evidence:** "Tonight I can write the saddest lines"
**Effect:** Emphasizes act of composition; makes poetry self-reflexive.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
