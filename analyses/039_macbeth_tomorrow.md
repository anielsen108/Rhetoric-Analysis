## PASSAGE

[L1] Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow,
[L2] Creeps in this petty pace from day to day,
[L3] To the last syllable of recorded time;
[L4] And all our yesterdays have lighted fools
[L5] The way to dusty death.

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Personification** (per-sah-nif-ih-KAY-shun)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** Attribution of human qualities to non-human things.
**Evidence:** "have lighted"
**Effect:** Makes time an active, almost malevolent agent.
**Near-miss:** Prosopopoeia goes further and gives the thing a speaking voice. Pathetic fallacy is personification of nature specifically, colored by the observer's emotion.

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "recorded time"
**Effect:** Transforms abstract time into visceral experience of futility.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.

### **Transferred Epithet** (trans-FERD EP-ih-thet)
**Lines:** L2
**Definition:** Adjective logically belonging to one noun applied to another.
**Evidence:** "petty pace"
**Effect:** Infects all of existence with Macbeth's nihilism.
**Near-miss:** A plain epithet sits on its rightful noun; hypallage is the deliberate misdelivery.

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Anaphora** (uh-NAF-or-uh)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Repetition at beginning of successive clauses.
**Evidence:** "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow"
**Effect:** Creates monotonous, defeated rhythm; suggests endless, meaningless repetition.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.

### **Polysyndeton** (pol-ee-SIN-duh-ton)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Use of multiple conjunctions.
**Evidence:** "and tomorrow, and tomorrow"
**Effect:** Slows pace; makes time feel heavy, dragging, oppressive.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.

## 2) SYNTAX AS STYLE (Tufte-grade)

**Sentence architecture:** The excerpt uses 5 displayed lines and 1 sentence movement. Its grammar distributes attention before, within, and after the main clauses instead of treating word order as a neutral container.

The form performs Macbeth's state: the repeated tomorrows that go nowhere, the slow creep of meaningless time, the syntax itself trapped in repetitive structures. This is grammar as existential crisis.
