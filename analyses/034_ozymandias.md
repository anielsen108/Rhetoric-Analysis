# Passage 034: "Ozymandias"

**Percy Bysshe Shelley** · *The Examiner* · 1818 | Selected passage

**Occasion/Pressure:** This lyric passage must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.

**Thesis of Effect:** Through frame narrative, present tense within past frame, and fragmented subjects, Shelley creates syntax that enacts the poem's theme. The broken syntax (fragments, dashes, ellipses) mirrors the broken statue. The form IS the decay it describes—syntax as…

## PASSAGE

[L1] I met a traveller from an antique land,
[L2] Who said—"Two vast and trunkless legs of stone
[L3] Stand in the desert...Near them, on the sand,
[L4] Half sunk a shattered visage lies

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Frame Narrative** (fraym NAIR-uh-tiv)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Story within a story.
**Evidence:** "traveller from an antique land, Who said"
**Effect:** Creates distance from the scene; suggests this is ancient, reported knowledge.
**Near-miss:** The label depends on the relation between “traveller from an antique land, Who said” and the surrounding sentence, not on the phrase in isolation.

### **Synecdoche** (sin-EK-duh-kee)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** Part representing whole.
**Evidence:** "visage"
**Effect:** Emphasizes fragmentation; the mighty are reduced to scattered parts.
**Near-miss:** A subtype of metonymy: synecdoche requires the part/whole relation specifically, not just any association.

### **Oxymoron** (ok-see-MOR-on)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** Combination of contradictory terms.
**Evidence:** "shattered visage"
**Effect:** Captures the contradiction of power that has fallen.
**Near-miss:** Paradox is a contradictory *statement* that unfolds; oxymoron is the two-word spark.

### **Hyperbole** (hy-PER-buh-lee)
**Lines:** L2
**Definition:** Deliberate exaggeration.
**Evidence:** "vast"
**Effect:** Emphasizes the scale of the tyrant's ambition and the scale of his fall.
**Near-miss:** Its opposite pole is understatement/litotes. When exaggeration collapses into the trivial for comic effect, that's bathos.

### **Irony** (EYE-ruh-nee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Expression opposite to literal meaning.
**Evidence:** "I met a traveller from an antique land"
**Effect:** Creates the poem's central theme: pride precedes fall, empires crumble.
**Near-miss:** Sarcasm is verbal irony weaponized at a target. Paradox asserts a real contradiction; irony merely means other than it says.

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Asyndeton** (uh-SIN-duh-ton / əˈsɪndətɒn)
**Lines:** L3
**Definition:** A sequence whose internal joins rely more on punctuation than repeated conjunctions.
**Evidence:** "Stand in the desert...Near them, on the sand,"
**Effect:** Comma pressure accelerates the sequence and lets the items arrive as one accumulating perception.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.

## 2) SYNTAX AS STYLE (Tufte-grade)

**Sentence architecture:** The excerpt uses 4 displayed lines and 1 sentence movement. Its grammar distributes attention before, within, and after the main clauses instead of treating word order as a neutral container.

### Opening Past Tense
"I met a traveller from an antique land"—simple **subject-verb-object** with prepositional phrase. The **past tense** "met" creates a chance encounter, suggesting the story's accidental discovery. The modifier "antique" (not "ancient") creates slightly elevated, even precious diction.

### Frame Device
"Who said—"—the **dash** after "said" introduces direct quotation. The syntax creates a frame: we're about to hear someone else's words, distancing us twice from the scene (speaker heard from traveller who saw the statue).

"Two vast and trunkless legs of stone"—the **subject** of the traveller's account is body parts, not a person. The syntax makes fragmentation the grammatical subject. The adjective "trunkless" (without body/torso) emphasizes incompleteness through negation.

### Simple Present
"Stand in the desert"—the **present tense** within the past-tense frame creates immediacy. The legs still stand, present tense, eternal. The syntax through tense shift makes the ruins contemporary.

### Spatial Relationship

### Participial Construction
"Half sunk"—the **past participle** without auxiliary creates compressed syntax. Not "is half sunk" but just "half sunk," making it adjectival, describing the visage's state.
