# Passage 052: "Master Harold"...and the Boys

## Author and Source
**Athol Fugard**, *"Master Harold"...and the Boys* (1982)

## Passage
> It was pointed at his trousers. "You can't do that anymore, Sam."
> "Do what, Hally?"
> "What you're doing now. Making me feel good."

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Understatement** (UN-der-state-ment)
*Definition:* Deliberate restraint in expression.
*Example:* "You can't do that anymore" for the end of their relationship.
*Effect:* Makes the tragedy more devastating through restraint.

### **Irony** (EYE-ruh-nee)
*Definition:* Meaning opposite to literal statement.
*Example:* "Making me feel good" as something prohibited by apartheid.
*Effect:* Exposes the cruelty of system that forbids kindness across racial lines.

### **Dialogue** (DY-uh-log)
*Definition:* Dramatic conversation.
*Example:* The exchange format itself.
*Effect:* Creates immediacy; we witness the heartbreaking moment.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Stage Direction
"It was pointed at his trousers"—the **passive construction** creates objective, almost clinical description. The pronoun "It" (Harold's gesture) is grammatical subject but not agent.

### Direct Speech
"You can't do that anymore, Sam"—the **modal negative "can't"** expresses prohibition, not inability. The syntax makes this about rules, not capacity.

The **vocative "Sam"** at end creates intimacy even as the content pushes Sam away.

### Question Format
"Do what, Hally?"—Sam's **question** uses **fronted object** ("Do what" rather than "What do [you mean]?"). The syntax is compressed, economical—Sam cuts to the point.

The **vocative "Hally"** (nickname, intimate) contrasts with what's coming.

### Gerund Phrase Answer
"What you're doing now. Making me feel good"—Harold's answer uses **demonstrative "What"** + relative clause, then **appositive gerund phrase** that specifies.

The syntax creates two-part revelation:
1. General: "What you're doing"
2. Specific: "Making me feel good"

### Present Progressive
"you're doing now"—the **present progressive** emphasizes ongoing action in this moment. The **temporal adverb "now"** stresses immediacy—not what you did before, but what you're doing RIGHT NOW.

### Infinitive Object
"Making me feel good"—the **gerund phrase** serving as appositive. The causative construction "make + object + adjective" (make me feel good) shows Sam's agency—he causes Harold's feelings.

The **adjective "good"** is simple, almost childlike. The syntax through simplicity makes the prohibition more cruel—something so simple, so benign, now forbidden.

### Elliptical Syntax
Much is unsaid. The syntax is spare, economical—typical of Fugard's style. What ISN'T said (apartheid, racism, the system) is present through absence.

### Stylistic Effect
Through simple syntax, economical dialogue, and understated language, Fugard creates devastating revelation. The form performs the content: apartheid makes even simple human kindness impossible. The stripped syntax—no elaboration, no rhetoric, just plain statement—makes the tragedy more powerful. This is grammar as heartbreak: syntax so simple it seems transparent, but carrying enormous weight. The very plainness of "making me feel good" as something now prohibited exposes the system's cruelty through understated, compressed grammar.
