# Passage 099: "Orientalism"

## Author and Source
**Edward Said**, *Orientalism* (1978)

## Passage
> The Orient was almost a European invention, and had been since antiquity a place of romance, exotic beings, haunting memories and landscapes, remarkable experiences.

## Rhetorical Tropes

### **Litotes** (LY-tuh-teez)
*Definition:* Understatement through negation.
*Example:* "almost a European invention"
*Effect:* Mutes radical claim while asserting it; "almost" admits partial reality while emphasizing construction.

### **Asyndeton** (uh-SIN-duh-tahn)
*Definition:* Omission of conjunctions between final items.
*Example:* "romance, exotic beings, haunting memories and landscapes, remarkable experiences"
*Effect:* Accumulates Orientalist fantasies rapidly; only final "and" before climax.

### **Alliteration** (uh-lit-uh-RAY-shun)
*Definition:* Repetition of initial consonant sounds.
*Example:* "haunting...had...histories"
*Effect:* Creates incantatory rhythm suggesting mythmaking.

## Syntax as Style Analysis

### Copulative Construction
"The Orient was...a European invention"—**subject-copula-complement** with definite articles. Syntax makes ontological claim.

### Hedging Adverb
"almost"—**adverb modifying complement**. Syntax softens while maintaining critique of constructed nature.

### Past Perfect Duration
"had been since antiquity"—**past perfect** with temporal phrase. Syntax emphasizes longstanding construction.

### Appositive Series
"a place of romance, exotic beings, haunting memories and landscapes, remarkable experiences"—**noun phrase** followed by **genitive series**. Syntax accumulates Orientalist projections.

### Adjectival Accumulation
"romance, exotic...haunting...remarkable"—**four adjectives/nouns** modified by evaluative terms. Syntax performs the romanticizing it critiques.

### Stylistic Effect
Through hedged copulative identification, past perfect emphasizing historical duration, and accumulating appositive series of romanticized qualities, Said creates syntax of ideological critique—grammar that exposes how "the Orient" is Western fantasy projected onto geography.
