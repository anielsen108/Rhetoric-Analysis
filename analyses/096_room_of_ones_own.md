# Passage 096: "A Room of One's Own"

**Virginia Woolf** · *A Room of One's Own* · 1929 | Selected passage

**Occasion/Pressure:** This expository argument must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.
**Persona/Stance:** A direct-address voice controls what the reader can know and how quickly the scene or proposition comes into focus.
**Thesis of Effect:** Through modal necessity, conditional syntax, parenthetical intimacy, and parallel construction linking woman and fiction, Woolf creates grammar of materialist feminism—syntax that grounds creativity in economics while acknowledging unresolved mysteries.

## PASSAGE

[L1] A woman must have money and a room of her own if she is to write fiction; and that, as you will see, leaves the great problem of the true nature of woman and the true nature of fiction unsolved.

**Near-miss:** Meiosis is plain understatement without the negation mechanism; litotes specifically runs on negatives.

### **Parenthesis** (puh-REN-thuh-sis)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Insertion of aside.
**Evidence:** "as you will see"
**Effect:** Creates conversational intimacy with reader.
**Near-miss:** An appositive renames the noun beside it; a parenthesis digresses without renaming anything.

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Parallelism** (pair-uh-LEL-iz-um)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Similar grammatical structures.
**Evidence:** "the true nature of woman and the true nature of fiction"
**Effect:** Links woman and fiction as equally mysterious, equally constructed.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
