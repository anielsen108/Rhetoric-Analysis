## PASSAGE

[L1] She was so evidently the victim of the civilization which had produced her, that the links of her bracelet seemed like manacles chaining her to her fate.

---

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Paradox** (PAIR-uh-doks)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Apparent contradiction.
**Evidence:** "produced"
**Effect:** Shows how society both creates and destroys its women.
**Near-miss:** Oxymoron is paradox compressed to a two-word collision ('living death'); antithesis balances opposites without asserting both are true.

### **Simile** (SIM-ih-lee)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Explicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "like manacles"
**Effect:** Transforms luxury into imprisonment.
**Near-miss:** Drop the 'like' and it becomes metaphor. An epic simile extends the comparison over many lines with its own internal scene.

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "She was so evidently the victim of the civilization which had produced"
**Effect:** Makes material privilege into captivity.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.
