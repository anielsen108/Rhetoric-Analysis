# Passage 108: "The Bishop"

**Victor Hugo** · *Les Misérables* · 1862 | translated from French)

**Occasion/Pressure:** This narrative passage must establish its controlling situation while making a small excerpt bear the work's larger pressure.
**Persona/Stance:** A third-person or impersonal voice controls what the reader can know and how quickly the scene or proposition comes into focus.
**Thesis of Effect:** Through extended conditional syntax, anaphoric insistence, tricolon catalog, and concluding litotes, Hugo creates grammar of prophetic social witness—syntax that demands justice while justifying literature's intervention.

---

## PASSAGE

[L1] So long as there shall exist, by reason of law and custom, a social condemnation, which, in the face of civilization, artificially creates hells on earth, and complicates a destiny that is divine with human fatality; so long as the three problems of the age—the degradation of man by poverty, the ruin of woman by starvation, and the dwarfing of childhood by physical and spiritual night—are not solved...books like this cannot be useless.

## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Metaphor** (MET-uh-for)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Implicit comparison.
**Evidence:** "hells on earth"
**Effect:** Makes abstract injustice vividly infernal.
**Near-miss:** Simile flags the comparison with like/as, keeping the two things separate. A metaphor sustained across a passage becomes extended metaphor or allegory; a strained, category-breaking one is catachresis.

### **Litotes** (LY-tuh-teez)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Understatement through negation.
**Evidence:** "cannot be useless"
**Effect:** Asserts book's necessity through double negative.
**Near-miss:** Meiosis is plain understatement without the negation mechanism; litotes specifically runs on negatives.
