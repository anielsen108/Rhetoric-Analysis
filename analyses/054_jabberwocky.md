## A) RHETORICAL TROPES USED

### **Neologism** (nee-AHL-oh-jiz-um)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** Invented words.
**Evidence:** "mome raths outgrabe"
**Effect:** Creates nonsense that somehow makes sense through syntax and sound.
**Near-miss:** Catachresis misuses an existing word; neologism mints a new one. A portmanteau is a neologism made by fusion (slithy = slimy + lithe).

### **Portmanteau** (port-man-TOH)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Word blending two words.
**Evidence:** "slithy"
**Effect:** Creates semantic compression; new words that suggest meanings.
**Near-miss:** The label depends on the relation between “slithy” and the surrounding sentence, not on the phrase in isolation.

### **Onomatopoeia** (on-uh-mat-uh-PEE-uh)
**Lines:** L4
**Definition:** Words that sound like what they mean.
**Evidence:** "outgrabe"
**Effect:** Makes nonsense words feel meaningful through sound.
**Near-miss:** Alliteration and assonance pattern sounds without imitating anything; onomatopoeia requires the sound-sense match.

## B) RHETORICAL SCHEMES USED

### **Polysyndeton** (pah-lee-SIN-duh-ton / ˌpɒlɪˈsɪndətɒn)
**Lines:** L1
**Definition:** Deliberate multiplication of conjunctions.
**Evidence:** "'Twas brillig, and the slithy toves"
**Effect:** The repeated conjunctions keep each item audible, turning sequence into accumulation rather than a compressed list.
**Risk:** Repeated mechanically, this pattern could become mannered; here its local placement keeps it functional.
