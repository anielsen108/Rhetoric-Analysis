2Syntax As Style (Tufte-grade)

Sentence architecture: The excerpt uses 1 displayed line and 1 sentence movement. Its grammar distributes attention before, within, and after the main clauses instead of treating word order as a neutral container.

Biblical Opening

"Four score and seven years ago"—this temporal phrase uses archaic measurement (score = 20 years). The syntax deliberately echoes King James Bible, giving the speech sacred resonance. Modern syntax would say "87 years ago," but Lincoln chooses elevated diction.

Subject-Verb-Object

"our fathers brought forth...a new nation"—the possessive "our fathers" (not "the founders") creates familial intimacy and collective ownership. The verb phrase "brought forth" continues Biblical register (suggesting birth). The object "a new nation" receives two parallel participial modifiers.

Parallel Participles

"conceived in Liberty, and dedicated to the proposition"—two past participles modify "nation":
- conceived in [abstraction]
- dedicated to [abstraction]

The syntax creates the nation as both naturally born (conceived) and purposefully created (dedicated)—dual origin.

Subordinate Noun Clause

"that all men are created equal"—this noun clause serves as object of "proposition." Lincoln embeds Jefferson's words syntactically, making the Declaration's principle the foundation (literally, in syntax) of the nation.

Stylistic Effect

Through archaic diction, biblical parallelism, and embedded quotation, Lincoln creates prose that sounds both ancient and urgent. The syntax performs a key rhetorical move: presenting 1863 America as fulfillment of 1776 promises. As Tufte notes, the elaborate syntax for a single sentence mirrors the complexity of preserving the Union—this isn't simple but requires careful, balanced construction.

Information flow: The opening establishes “Four score and seven years ago our fathers brought forth on this continent, a new nation,…”; subsequent material converts that initial footing into the new emphasis carried by “Four score and seven years ago our fathers brought forth on this continent, a new nation,….”

Faultlines
