3Deixis, Aspect, Modality

Deictic center: The main orienting terms are “i”, “she”, “it”. They locate the implied speaker, audience, scene, or temporal vantage from which the passage becomes intelligible.

Aspect and tense: Past-tense signals (“was”, “saw”, “went”) place the represented action behind the speaking moment.

Modality: The excerpt uses no explicit modal auxiliary. This matters because modality marks whether a proposition is asserted as fact, entertained as possibility, imposed as duty, or projected as intention.

Authority effect: The grammar of person, time, and possibility quietly establishes who may name reality and how firmly the reader is asked to accept that naming.

8Hotspots & Faultlines

Hotspots

  1. “smitten” — Litotes. Uses mild word for predatory attraction.
  2. “On our wedding day I was forty-six” — Asyndeton. Creates staccato, memory-fragment quality.
  3. “I went” — Anaphora. Foregrounds narrator's subjectivity, his story.

Faultlines

  1. Overstatement risk: Making the litotes more explicit would explain the phrase at the cost of the inference the reader currently performs.
  2. Pattern risk: Extending asyndeton beyond its present span could turn designed emphasis into mannerism.
  3. Paraphrase risk: Replacing “smitten” with its abstract meaning would preserve information but remove voice, rhythm, and the passage's interpretive invitation.
