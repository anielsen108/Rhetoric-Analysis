Stylistic Effect

Through tense uncertainty, rhetorical questioning of idiom, and syntactic reversal of rising/falling, Atwood creates grammar of resistance—language that questions even basic metaphors, refusing to accept conventional descriptions of reality.

Information flow: The opening establishes “Night falls.”; subsequent material converts that initial footing into the new emphasis carried by “Yet if you look east, at sunset, you can see night rising, not falling; darkness lifting….”

Micro-rewrites

3Deixis, Aspect, Modality

Deictic center: The main orienting terms are “it”, “that”, “you”. They locate the implied speaker, audience, scene, or temporal vantage from which the passage becomes intelligible.

Aspect and tense: The excerpt relies more on lexical verbs and states than on an explicitly marked retrospective frame.

Modality: The excerpt uses “can”. This matters because modality marks whether a proposition is asserted as fact, entertained as possibility, imposed as duty, or projected as intention.
