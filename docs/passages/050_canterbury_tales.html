2Syntax As Style (Tufte-grade)

Temporal Subordinate Clause

"Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote"—the temporal conjunction "Whan that" (When that) introduces subordinate clause. Middle English uses "that" where Modern English would omit it.

The subject "Aprill" with possessive "his" (April is masculine) and object "shoures soote" (sweet showers) creates the agent.

The adjective following noun ("shoures soote" = sweet showers) is both Middle English word order and creates emphasis on "soote."

Perfect Tense

"hath perced"—the present perfect "hath perced" (has pierced) uses Middle English auxiliary. The verb "pierced" is violent for what should be gentle spring rain—syntax makes nature aggressive.

Object of Piercing

"The droghte of March...to the roote"—the object is "the drought of March" (accumulated dryness), and the prepositional phrase "to the roote" specifies how deep the piercing goes. The syntax creates complete penetration—to the very root.

Coordinate Addition

"And bathed every veyne"—the coordinating conjunction adds second action. The verb shifts from violent (pierced) to gentle (bathed). The syntax through verb choice shows rain both breaks drought and nourishes.

The noun "veyne" (vein) makes botanical conduits anatomical—plants have circulatory systems like animals.

Prepositional Phrase

"in swich licour"—the phrase "in such liquor" specifies what bathes the veins. "Licour" (liquor/liquid) makes rainwater almost medicinal or alchemical.

Relative Clause

"Of which vertu engendred is the flour"—this relative clause explains the liquor's power. The syntax inverts normal order: "engendred is" instead of "is engendred." This inversion is both poetic and emphatic.

The abstract noun "vertu" (virtue/power) suggests almost magical property. The verb "engendred" (engendered/created) makes plant growth seem like procreation.
