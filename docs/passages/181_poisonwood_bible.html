Stylistic Effect

Through imperative imagine/picture commands, indefinite ruin with paradoxical must-never-have-happened, declarative I-want-you, possessive its-conscience assignment, and appositive eyes-in-trees metaphor, Kingsolver creates syntax of readerly conscription—grammar that commands audience into specific position as embedded moral witness to impossible catastrophe.

Information flow: The opening establishes “Imagine a ruin so strange it must never have happened.”; subsequent material converts that initial footing into the new emphasis carried by “I want you to be its conscience, the eyes in the trees..”

Micro-rewrites

Compression test: Reduce the excerpt to one independent clause. The informational core remains, but the pacing, qualification, and hierarchy identified above disappear.

Expansion test: Convert its modifiers into separate sentences. Clarity may increase locally, but the original relations among perceptions, actions, and judgments become less simultaneous.

Modality: The excerpt uses “must”. This matters because modality marks whether a proposition is asserted as fact, entertained as possibility, imposed as duty, or projected as intention.

8Hotspots & Faultlines

Hotspots

  1. “I want you to be” — Apostrophe. Creates intimate, commanding relationship with reader.
  2. “eyes in the trees” — Metaphor. Makes reader into moral witness embedded in landscape.
  3. “so strange it must never have happened” — Paradox. Suggests trauma too extreme to be credible.

Faultlines

  1. Overstatement risk: Making the apostrophe more explicit would explain the phrase at the cost of the inference the reader currently performs.
  2. Pattern risk: Extending metaphor beyond its present span could turn designed emphasis into mannerism.
  3. Paraphrase risk: Replacing “I want you to be” with its abstract meaning would preserve information but remove voice, rhythm, and the passage's interpretive invitation.
