Stylistic Effect

Through simple past false certainty, immediate disjunctive maybe, epistemic I-don't-know, telegraphic abbreviated institutional language, dismissive that-means-nothing, and circular maybe-yesterday return, Camus creates syntax of absurdist alienation—grammar showing Meursault's radical disconnection where even mother's death cannot generate clear knowledge or appropriate feeling.

Information flow: The opening establishes “Maman died today.”; subsequent material converts that initial footing into the new emphasis carried by “Maybe it was yesterday..”

Micro-rewrites

Compression test: Reduce the excerpt to one independent clause. The informational core remains, but the pacing, qualification, and hierarchy identified above disappear.

Expansion test: Convert its modifiers into separate sentences. Clarity may increase locally, but the original relations among perceptions, actions, and judgments become less simultaneous.

Deictic center: The main orienting terms are “i”, “that”, “it”. They locate the implied speaker, audience, scene, or temporal vantage from which the passage becomes intelligible.

Aspect and tense: Past-tense signals (“was”) place the represented action behind the speaking moment.

Modality: The excerpt uses no explicit modal auxiliary. This matters because modality marks whether a proposition is asserted as fact, entertained as possibility, imposed as duty, or projected as intention.

Authority effect: The grammar of person, time, and possibility quietly establishes who may name reality and how firmly the reader is asked to accept that naming.
