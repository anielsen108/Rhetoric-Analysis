Stylistic Effect

Through inverted copulative tautology, interrupted attribution, relative what-you're-used-to definition, demonstrative this-horror, modal may-not-seem acknowledgment, vague after-a-time, and double future will/will-become inevitability, Atwood creates syntax of totalitarian normalization—grammar showing how authority redefines ordinary through circular logic and temporal inevitability, making horror mundane through linguistic assertion.

Information flow: The opening establishes “Ordinary, said Aunt Lydia, is what you are used to.”; subsequent material converts that initial footing into the new emphasis carried by “It will become ordinary..”

Micro-rewrites

Compression test: Reduce the excerpt to one independent clause. The informational core remains, but the pacing, qualification, and hierarchy identified above disappear.

Expansion test: Convert its modifiers into separate sentences. Clarity may increase locally, but the original relations among perceptions, actions, and judgments become less simultaneous.

3Deixis, Aspect, Modality

Deictic center: The main orienting terms are “you”, “this”, “now”, “it”. They locate the implied speaker, audience, scene, or temporal vantage from which the passage becomes intelligible.

Aspect and tense: Past-tense signals (“said”) place the represented action behind the speaking moment.

Modality: The excerpt uses “may”, “will”. This matters because modality marks whether a proposition is asserted as fact, entertained as possibility, imposed as duty, or projected as intention.

Authority effect: The grammar of person, time, and possibility quietly establishes who may name reality and how firmly the reader is asked to accept that naming.

8Hotspots & Faultlines

Hotspots

  1. “is what you are used to” — Tautology. Creates circular definition revealing normalization process.
  2. “Ordinary” — Euphemism. Obscures horror through bland language.
  3. “It will become” — Anaphora. Insists on inevitability of adaptation.

Faultlines

  1. Overstatement risk: Making the tautology more explicit would explain the phrase at the cost of the inference the reader currently performs.
  2. Pattern risk: Extending euphemism beyond its present span could turn designed emphasis into mannerism.
  3. Paraphrase risk: Replacing “is what you are used to” with its abstract meaning would preserve information but remove voice, rhythm, and the passage's interpretive invitation.
