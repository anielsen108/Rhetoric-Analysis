Syntax

Triple Repetition

"Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow"—the three-fold repetition with polysyndeton creates what Tufte calls syntactic monotony-as-meaning. The form IS the futility.

Present Tense Verb

"Creeps"—shifts from future (tomorrow) to present tense, suggesting all time creeps, always. The verb choice "creeps" (not "moves" or "goes") emphasizes slowness, stealthiness, something unpleasant.

Prepositional Phrase Chain

"in this petty pace from day to day"—stacked prepositional phrases create the sense of being trapped in syntactic structure itself, moving from one preposition to another with no escape.

Coordinating Turn

"And all our yesterdays"—the coordinating conjunction shifts from future (tomorrows) to past (yesterdays), suggesting time surrounds us from both directions.

Perfect Tense

"have lighted"—the present perfect connects past action to present consequence. The past has created our current situation: leading to death.

Object and Destination

"lighted fools / The way to dusty death"—the syntax makes "fools" the object (we are the fools) and "way to dusty death" the destination. The adjective "dusty" makes death not glorious but dirty, dry, crumbling.

Stylistic Effect

Through anaphoric repetition, polysyndeton, and tense shifts, Shakespeare creates syntax of nihilistic despair. The form performs Macbeth's state: the repeated tomorrows that go nowhere, the slow creep of meaningless time, the syntax itself trapped in repetitive structures. This is grammar as existential crisis.

Information flow: The opening establishes “Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow, Creeps in this petty pace from day to day, To the…”; subsequent material converts that initial footing into the new emphasis carried by “Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow, Creeps in this petty pace from day to day, To the….”
